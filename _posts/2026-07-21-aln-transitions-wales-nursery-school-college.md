A child’s educational journey should not be decided in one pass.

Not at nursery.

Not at school entry.

Not at primary to secondary transition.

Not at GCSE age.

Not at the move to college.

Not at 16.

And not because one plan, written at one point in time, happened to catch the child on a good day or a bad one.

Real lives do not map neatly onto one route.

A child may be in health crisis and later recover.

A young person may have a degenerative condition and need more physical, health or cognitive support over time.

A bereavement may cause emotional regression, distress, anxiety, attendance problems or loss of confidence for a period. With the right support, that same child may later recover and exceed expectations made during the crisis.

A family may need to move between local authority areas because of work, housing, safety, family support or health reasons.

A child may need mainstream school, then flexi-schooling, then EOTAS, then a careful return to mainstream.

Another child may need specialist teaching facilities for a period, then a more blended route later.

The education system needs to be able to respond to real life, not only the formal stage gates of nursery, primary, secondary and further education.

This article explains ALN transitions in Wales, and why transition planning should be flexible, evidence-informed and responsive when a child or young person’s needs, health, family circumstances or confidence change.

This article provides general information only. It is not legal advice, clinical advice, safeguarding advice or Tribunal representation.

## Quick answer

ALN transition planning is not only about moving from one setting to another.

It should also consider changes in:

- the child or young person’s health;
- emotional well-being;
- family circumstances;
- communication needs;
- sensory needs;
- mobility or physical access;
- confidence;
- attendance;
- placement;
- home education status;
- EOTAS arrangements;
- transport, including whether the journey is realistic and safe;
- social care;
- post-16 plans;
- the young person’s own views and consent.

Welsh Government’s Parent and Carer Toolkit says ALNCos work across all stages of learning, including moving to a new year group or school, and planning for life after school, such as college, training or work [1].

That matters.

But families often need more than a formal transition meeting.

They need a system that can notice when the current route is no longer working, review the evidence, adjust the plan, and help the child or young person move without unnecessary crisis or loss of education.

## Transitions are not one-off events

A formal transition point might be:

- nursery to reception;
- primary to secondary;
- Year 9 or Year 10 options;
- school to further education;
- college to training, work or supported progression.

Those points matter.

But they are not the only transitions families face.

There can also be hidden transitions.

For example:

- moving from full-time attendance to reduced attendance;
- moving from mainstream to specialist provision;
- moving from specialist provision back towards mainstream;
- moving from school to EOTAS;
- moving from EOTAS back into school or college;
- moving from school attendance to elective home education;
- moving from elective home education back into school or college;
- moving from parent-led advocacy to young person self-advocacy;
- moving from one local authority area to another;
- moving from child services to adult-facing services.

These are real transitions too.

They can affect education, health, social care, transport, confidence and family stability.

If the system only notices formal stage gates, it will miss too much.

## A child’s route should be able to change

A child’s needs are not frozen at the point their IDP is written.

Some needs become clearer over time.

Some reduce with good support.

Some increase because of health, development, trauma, sensory overload, anxiety, puberty, mental health, physical illness, bereavement or family instability.

Some children mask for years and then crash.

Some children appear to regress during crisis, then recover strongly once the pressure reduces.

That does not mean the original picture was wrong.

It means the child was changing, and the system needed to keep up.

Welsh Government’s ALN decision-making guidance says decisions should be evidence-informed, focused on the learner’s needs, clearly explained, and based on robust information [2].

That principle should apply during transition too.

A child should not be trapped in an old view of who they are.

A young person should not be held back by predictions made during their worst period.

And a family should not be told “this is the route now” as though no future movement is possible.

## When transition planning should be reviewed

Transition planning should not wait for the next formal stage gate if circumstances have already changed.

Parents may need to ask for a review where:

- attendance changes;
- health needs change;
- anxiety or distress increases;
- the child stops coping with the current setting;
- ALP is not being delivered;
- a professional report gives new evidence;
- the family moves area;
- transport becomes unsafe or unrealistic;
- the young person’s views change;
- a planned move starts to break down.

A useful question is:

> What has changed since this plan was agreed, and does the IDP or transition plan still match the child’s current needs?

## Deferred entry and adjusted starts

Some children are not ready for the standard pace of nursery or school entry.

That may be because of development, health, disability, trauma, prematurity, anxiety, sensory needs, communication needs or family crisis.

Parents may want to explore whether entry can be deferred, delayed, part-time, phased or adjusted.

This needs careful local advice because admissions rules matter.

Welsh Government’s school admissions FAQ says that, before compulsory school age, parents can ask the admission authority whether their child can go to school part-time or put off starting school until later in the school year. This is known as deferred entry [6].

That is not the same as saying every child has an automatic right to repeat or shift an entire school year.

But the wider principle matters.

School entry should not be treated as a cliff edge.

Where a child needs a slower or more supported start, families should be able to ask what options exist, what evidence is needed, and how ALN, health, early years and admissions staff will work together.

A useful question is:

> Does my child need a standard start, a phased start, deferred entry, adjusted attendance, early years ALN input, health advice, or a different setting discussion?

## Flexi-schooling and movement back into mainstream

Some children need a bridge back into school.

Welsh Government’s elective home education guidance says flexi-schooling can be beneficial where a child is transitioning back into school, transitioning into school for the first time, has autism and finds school challenging, is suffering from school phobia or anxiety, or has an illness [5].

The same guidance explains that flexi-schooling is different from home education. The child remains on the school roll but attends school part-time, while being educated at home for the rest of the time [5].

This matters because some families are wrongly offered only two choices:

> full-time school or full-time home education

For some children, neither is the right answer.

A child recovering from illness, anxiety, bullying, sensory overload or unmet ALN may need a staged return.

That does not mean the family has failed.

It may mean the system needs to be more flexible while the child rebuilds trust, stamina and confidence.

Flexi-schooling is not guaranteed in every case. Welsh Government guidance says local authorities are encouraged to work with headteachers to consider whether a school can facilitate flexi-schooling where it is in the child’s best interests [5].

So parents should ask clearly:

- Is a phased return possible?
- Is flexi-schooling possible?
- What would the child do in school?
- What would happen at home?
- How would attendance be recorded?
- How would ALP be delivered?
- When would the arrangement be reviewed?
- What would success look like?
- What happens if the child needs more time?

## Mainstream, specialist provision and specialist teaching facilities

Some children thrive in mainstream with the right ALP.

Some need specialist teaching facilities or specialist provision.

Some may need specialist support for a period and then move again later.

The important point is that placement should not be treated as a permanent label on the child.

A child may move towards specialist support because mainstream is not currently meeting need.

Another child may move back towards mainstream because health, communication, confidence or regulation has improved.

A young person may need a blended route, with some specialist input and some mainstream or community-based learning.

Families should not be made to feel that one move decides everything.

The better question is:

> What setting, support, teaching approach and health or social care input does this child need now, and how will we review whether that is still right later?

## EOTAS should not be treated as a dead end

Education Otherwise Than At School, or EOTAS, can be vital where a child cannot attend school and still needs suitable education.

Welsh Government’s toolkit defines an education setting broadly, including nurseries, schools, colleges and other arrangements such as EOTAS [1].

Estyn’s 2026 report on EOTAS tuition in Wales says the strongest authorities view tuition as purposeful and, where appropriate, time-limited support that helps pupils sustain or re-engage with learning and prepare for future pathways [7].

That is the right principle.

EOTAS should not be a holding pen.

It should not be where ambition disappears.

It should not mean a child quietly loses access to qualifications, specialist input, health support or progression planning.

For some children, EOTAS may be a short-term bridge back into school.

For others, it may be part of a longer route.

For some young people, the right next step may be college, training, supported volunteering, Bridge Pathway 1, or another progression route.

The key point is this:

> EOTAS should have a purpose, a plan, and a route onwards.

## Home education can also change over time

Some families choose elective home education as a positive, informed decision.

Some move into home education because school has become impossible.

Some do both: they begin from crisis, then rebuild learning in a way that works for the child.

Welsh Government’s elective home education guidance recognises that home educating families are diverse and may choose home education for many reasons, including health, emotional well-being, bullying, additional educational provision requirements, language choice, flexibility and tailoring of approach [5].

The same guidance says there is no one-size-fits-all approach to home education, and that home education can vary over time and subject [5].

That flexibility matters.

But home education should never be used to hide unmet ALN.

Welsh Government’s guidance says where it appears that a home-educated child may have ALN, the local authority must decide whether the child has ALN. If the child has ALN, the local authority must prepare and maintain an IDP and secure the ALP described in that plan [5].

So families should know both things:

- elective home education can be a valid route;
- a child does not lose ALN rights simply because they are educated at home.

## Moving between local authority areas

Families move.

They may move because of work, housing, domestic safety, family support, medical treatment, transport, finances, or the need to be closer to services.

They may move within Wales.

They may move between Wales and England.

That can affect ALN, health, social care, transport and education routes.

The move itself can also affect the child.

A child may lose relationships, routines, familiar spaces and trusted adults.

A young person may need time to rebuild confidence and explain their support needs again.

Transport can become a transition issue in its own right. A placement may look suitable on paper, but the journey, timing, anxiety, fatigue, supervision or medical needs may make it harder in practice.

Parents should keep copies of:

- the current IDP;
- professional reports;
- health letters;
- social care assessments;
- school or college correspondence;
- attendance records;
- transport decisions;
- meeting notes;
- examples of what has and has not worked;
- the young person’s own views;
- any signed consent letter if the young person wants a parent or trusted adult to support them.

A useful question is:

> Who is responsible for making sure my child’s support transfers safely, and what information will be shared before the move happens?

## Post-16 transition is not just a college form

The move from school to further education can be one of the riskiest points for young people with ALN.

At 16, legal rights, consent, expectations and services can all shift.

Welsh Government’s parent rights guide says young people up to age 25 with ALN are entitled to an IDP if they stay in education [3].

It also explains that when a child becomes a young person, ALN rights are given to the young person, rather than to the parent in the same way [3].

That means post-16 planning needs to include the young person properly.

It also needs to recognise that transition is emotional, not only administrative.

But it should not abandon them to manage everything alone.

Self-advocacy is a skill.

Like any skill, it often develops through practice, support and confidence over time.

A young person may need help to:

- understand their IDP;
- visit a college;
- ask what support is available;
- explain what helps;
- understand transport options;
- talk about health or social care needs;
- decide whether a parent or trusted adult can support them;
- prepare for meetings;
- keep records;
- build confidence before formal education, training or work.

A signed consent letter can help where a young person with capacity wants a parent, carer or trusted adult to support them in discussions with education, social care or transport services.

The aim is not to take over.

The aim is to taper support while the young person builds confidence.

## Bridge Pathway 1 and fragile transitions

Some young people are not ready to move straight from education disruption into college, training or work.

That does not mean they have no future.

It may mean they need a bridge.

Learn Without Limits CIC created Bridge Pathway 1 opportunities to help young people build confidence, contribute to real projects, develop evidence of skills, and practise communicating what support helps them [8].

Bridge Pathway 1 is not a replacement for education, ALN support, health services, social care or specialist advice.

It is a practical progression route where confidence, experience and self-advocacy need rebuilding.

For some young people, that can make the difference between:

> I cannot do anything

and:

> I have done something real, I know what helps, and I can talk about what I might try next.

That matters because transition is not only about placement.

It is also about confidence, identity and hope.

## What parents can ask at any transition point

At any transition point, parents can ask:

- What is changing?
- What evidence has been reviewed?
- What does the child or young person say?
- What does the family say?
- What is working now?
- What is not working?
- Has the IDP been reviewed?
- Is the ALP still right?
- Is the ALP specific enough?
- Who will deliver the support after the transition?
- Is health advice needed?
- Is social care involved?
- Is transport needed?
- Is Welsh-medium ALP needed?
- Is the young person over compulsory school age?
- Is consent needed for a parent or trusted adult to support them?
- What happens if this route does not work?
- When will the plan be reviewed again?

The most important question may be:

> What is the fallback plan if this transition starts to break down?

Families should not have to wait until the child has lost education, confidence or health before anyone agrees to review the plan.

## What LWL believes

Learn Without Limits CIC does not believe a child’s full educational journey is decided in one pass at any stage.

A plan that worked last year may not work now.

A placement that was right during crisis may not be right after recovery.

A child who needed less support may now need more, and a young person who once needed intensive support may later need a route back towards independence.

The system should be able to respond.

That means Wales needs transitions that are:

- flexible;
- evidence-informed;
- child-centred;
- young-person-centred;
- realistic about health and family life;
- able to move between routes;
- reviewed when circumstances change;
- focused on prevention, not crisis response.

A child should not have to fail in one setting before another route is discussed.

A young person should not have to lose confidence before support is adjusted.

A parent should not have to lose income or stability at home before the system accepts that a plan is not working.

Transitions are not just administrative handovers.

They are prevention points.

Used well, they can keep a child connected to learning.

Used badly, they can become the point where a child falls out of education.

## Final thought

The formal stage gates matter.

Nursery to school matters.

Primary to secondary matters.

School to college matters.

But real lives do not always follow those neat lines.

A child may need to pause, return, step sideways, recover, rebuild, move setting, change support, increase specialist input, reduce specialist input, or try a bridge route before moving forward again.

That is not failure.

That is life.

The ALN system should be able to respond to the child in front of it now, not only the route someone expected them to follow last year.
