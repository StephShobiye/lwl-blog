<h1 data-end="1497" data-start="1462"><strong data-end="1495" data-start="1464">What masking actually is</strong></h1>
<p data-end="1603" data-start="1498">Masking means hiding distress, copying peers, and suppressing natural reactions to appear “fine”.</p>
<p data-end="1627" data-start="1605">A child who masks may:</p>
<ul data-end="1856" data-start="1629">
<li data-end="1650" data-start="1629">
<p data-end="1650" data-start="1631">force eye contact</p>
</li>
<li data-end="1685" data-start="1651">
<p data-end="1685" data-start="1653">stay silent even when confused</p>
</li>
<li data-end="1707" data-start="1686">
<p data-end="1707" data-start="1688">suppress stimming</p>
</li>
<li data-end="1736" data-start="1708">
<p data-end="1736" data-start="1710">copy voices and gestures</p>
</li>
<li data-end="1771" data-start="1737">
<p data-end="1771" data-start="1739">freeze during sensory overload</p>
</li>
<li data-end="1809" data-start="1772">
<p data-end="1809" data-start="1774">hide their needs to avoid trouble</p>
</li>
<li data-end="1856" data-start="1810">
<p data-end="1856" data-start="1812">pretend to be calm when they feel panicked</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p data-end="1923" data-start="1858">Masking is not a sign of resilience.<br data-end="1897" data-start="1894" />
It is a survival strategy.</p>

<h1 data-end="3643" data-start="3541"><strong data-end="3641" data-start="3545">Masking and code switching — the hidden double pressure for many minority ethnic learners</strong></h1>
<p data-end="3744" data-start="3644">Masking is one layer of effort.<br data-end="3678" data-start="3675" />
Some children carry a second, invisible layer: <strong data-end="3743" data-start="3725"><a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=code+switching+definition&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=3645572001503185699" target="_blank">code switching</a></strong>.</p>
<p data-end="3871" data-start="3746">Code switching is when a person changes their speech, behaviour, or cultural expressions to fit into the dominant environment.</p>
<p data-end="3939" data-start="3873">The easiest way to understand this is through everyday experience:</p>
<ul data-end="4051" data-start="3941">
<li data-end="3992" data-start="3941">
<p data-end="3992" data-start="3943">You speak one way when your boss’s boss phones.</p>
</li>
<li data-end="4051" data-start="3993">
<p data-end="4051" data-start="3995">You speak another way when your closest friend phones.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p data-end="4099" data-start="4053">Both are “you”, but you adjust to the context.</p>
<p data-end="4204" data-start="4101">For many minority ethnic children, code switching is a more intense version of this.<br data-end="4188" data-start="4185" />
They may adjust:</p>
<ul data-end="4349" data-start="4206">
<li data-end="4222" data-start="4206">
<p data-end="4222" data-start="4208">their accent</p>
</li>
<li data-end="4237" data-start="4223">
<p data-end="4237" data-start="4225">their tone</p>
</li>
<li data-end="4266" data-start="4238">
<p data-end="4266" data-start="4240">their rhythm of speaking</p>
</li>
<li data-end="4288" data-start="4267">
<p data-end="4288" data-start="4269">their expressions</p>
</li>
<li data-end="4312" data-start="4289">
<p data-end="4312" data-start="4291">their body language</p>
</li>
<li data-end="4349" data-start="4313">
<p data-end="4349" data-start="4315">parts of their cultural identity</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p data-end="4430" data-start="4351">Not because they want to “blend in”, but because they do not want to stand out and stay safe. It is a survival strategy.</p>
<p data-end="5162" data-start="4528">Code switching can also occur in a completely different context here in Wales. Some rural Welsh speaking children are placed in urban English-speaking ALN units, often far from home. These young people may unconsciously soften their Welsh accent, avoid familiar expressions, or adjust their speech patterns to match their classmates and staff. None of this is anyone’s fault. It is simply what many children feel they must do to fit in with their environment. For a <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=define+neurodivergent&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=3645572001503185699" target="_blank">neurodivergent</a> child who is already masking sensory or emotional overwhelm, this additional shift in language and identity can increase the strain of the day even further.</p>

</li>
<li data-end="5389" data-start="5360">
<p data-end="5389" data-start="5362">“Do I look out of place?”</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p data-end="5447" data-start="5391">This is cognitive and emotional work that <em data-end="5446" data-start="5433">never stops</em>.</p>

<ol data-end="5646" data-start="5533">

<li data-end="5701" data-start="5688">

</li>
<li data-end="5740" data-start="5729">
<p data-end="5740" data-start="5731">anxiety</p>
</li>
<li data-end="5760" data-start="5741">
<p data-end="5760" data-start="5743"><a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=identity+strain+definition&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=3645572001503185699" target="_blank">identity strain</a></p>
</li>
<li data-end="5801" data-start="5761">
<p data-end="5801" data-start="5763">masking so deep it becomes automatic</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p data-end="5861" data-start="5803">It is no wonder these children unravel when they get home.</p>

<p data-end="6799" data-start="6745">If distress appears only at home, some schools assume:</p>

<h1 data-end="7100" data-start="7082"><strong data-end="7098" data-start="7084">Conclusion</strong></h1>
<p data-end="7328" data-start="7101">No child should have to work so hard to appear calm that they collapse the moment they get home.<br data-end="7200" data-start="7197" />
Masking and code switching place enormous emotional strain on young people, especially those who are neurodivergent or disabled.</p>
<p data-end="7395" data-start="7330">Welsh families deserve recognition, compassion and early support.</p>
