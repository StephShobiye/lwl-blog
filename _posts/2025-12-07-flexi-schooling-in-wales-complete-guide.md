Flexi-Schooling in Wales: A Complete Guide for Parents
A Learn Without Limits CIC guide
Flexi-schooling is one of the most misunderstood areas of education in Wales.
Parents are told flexi-schooling is illegal. Schools confuse it with reduced timetables. Local authority officers sometimes misunderstand it completely.
Yet flexi-schooling is legal, well-established, and used by families across Wales.
Learn Without Limits CIC has supported parents with flexi-schooling for more than a decade.
This guide sets the record straight with clear explanations, Welsh legal references, scripts and letter templates.
This article provides general information only. It is not legal advice, safeguarding advice, attendance advice or Tribunal representation.
Quick answer
Flexi-schooling is an arrangement where a child stays registered at school but is educated partly at school and partly at home.
It is different from:
elective home education;
Education Otherwise Than At School, or EOTAS;
a reduced timetable;
a school telling a parent to keep a child at home.
Flexi-schooling should be parent-requested and headteacher-agreed.
It should not be used by a school to avoid ALN duties, reduce attendance pressure, avoid providing support, or push a child out of school.
What flexi-schooling actually is
Flexi-schooling is an arrangement where:
the parent chooses to educate the child part-time at home;
the child remains registered at the school;
the headteacher agrees to the arrangement;
the school provides part of the education;
the parent provides the rest;
attendance for agreed home-learning time is authorised and recorded appropriately.
Flexi-schooling is a lawful and flexible educational choice.
It is not deregistration, not EOTAS and not a reduced timetable.
What flexi-schooling is not
Understanding what flexi-schooling is not helps prevent confusion.
Not a reduced timetable
A reduced timetable is usually:
initiated by the school;
temporary;
used for short-term support;
reviewed regularly;
intended to support reintegration or manage a specific short-term situation.
A reduced timetable should not be presented as flexi-schooling.
Flexi-schooling is parent-requested. A reduced timetable is school-led.
Those are not the same thing.
Not EOTAS
EOTAS is education arranged by the local authority for a child who cannot attend school.
Flexi-schooling is different.
With flexi-schooling, the child remains on the school roll and attends school for part of the week.
Not school-directed part-time attendance
A school cannot direct a parent to educate their child at home.
If a school pressures a family to remove a child from school or take on education at home, that may become an off-rolling concern.
Welsh Government’s elective home education guidance includes a section on off-rolling and says there is anecdotal evidence of schools putting pressure on parents to home educate [1].
Not elective home education
With flexi-schooling the child:
stays on roll;
keeps their school place;
keeps the school’s ALN duties;
remains within the school attendance and safeguarding system.
That is very different from elective home education.
Welsh Government’s elective home education guidance says flexi-schooling is distinct from home education [1].
The legal framework in Wales
Education Act 1996, Section 7
Section 7 of the Education Act 1996 says parents must cause their child to receive efficient full-time education suitable to their age, ability, aptitude and any additional learning needs they may have, either by regular attendance at school or otherwise [1].
Flexi-schooling sits within that wider “at school or otherwise” principle, but it still depends on the school agreeing to the arrangement while the child remains on roll.
Welsh Government elective home education guidance
Welsh Government’s elective home education guidance explains that flexi-schooling is distinct from home education [1].
It says a flexi-schooling arrangement can only happen with school agreement, and that the parent takes responsibility for the education provided away from school under the agreed arrangement [1].
Attendance coding
Schools must record attendance using the appropriate Welsh attendance codes.
Welsh Government attendance material has referred to Code C for agreed flexi-schooling where the school has agreed that a pupil is receiving part of their education at home [3].
Families should ask the school to confirm the attendance code in writing.
They should also check that the code is authorised and does not wrongly record agreed home-learning time as unauthorised absence.
Safeguarding
Schools retain safeguarding responsibilities for pupils on roll.
Flexi-schooling should not be used to make a struggling child less visible.
Where a child is vulnerable, the arrangement should be clear, recorded, reviewed and understood by the adults involved.
ALN law
Flexi-schooling does not remove duties under the Additional Learning Needs and Education Tribunal (Wales) Act 2018 or the ALN Code for Wales.
If a child has an IDP, the school and local authority still need to understand how Additional Learning Provision, or ALP, will be delivered.
Welsh Government’s ALN and elective home education guidance says that when a parent requests flexi-schooling, a headteacher should consider the parent’s ability to deliver any ALP at home [2].
That is an important point for ALN families.
Flexi-schooling should not make ALP vague, hidden or impossible to deliver.
A school cannot use flexi-schooling to avoid support
Flexi-schooling should be parent-requested and headteacher-agreed.
A school may agree or refuse.
But a school should not:
suggest flexi-schooling to reduce attendance pressure;
offer it as an alternative to ALN provision;
encourage parents to educate part-time at home because support is not in place;
pressure families into agreeing;
use it to avoid providing support;
use it to make a child less visible.
If a school suggests flexi-schooling as a way to remove a difficult problem from school, parents should be cautious.
The better question is:
> Is this genuinely a parent-requested flexi-schooling arrangement, or is the school trying to avoid providing education, ALN support or safeguarding oversight?

When flexi-schooling may be appropriate
Flexi-schooling may be considered where part-time school attendance and part-time home education genuinely help the child access education.
Example 1: Long Covid fatigue
A child has severe fatigue and relapses after full school days.
Morning school attendance plus home learning in the afternoon may reduce crashes and maintain progress.
Example 2: Autistic sensory overload
A child becomes distressed after lunch because of sensory overload.
Afternoon home learning may protect wellbeing and support better engagement during the time they are in school.
Example 3: Demand-related distress
Full school days trigger shutdowns, meltdowns or severe distress.
A carefully agreed flexi-schooling plan may reduce pressure while adults work out what support is needed.
Example 4: ADHD and pacing
A child may manage focused mornings in school but need structured afternoons at home to regulate, move, rest or learn differently.
Flexi-schooling should still be planned, reviewed and connected to the child’s education.
Flexi-schooling does not have to be forever
Flexi-schooling is a tool, not a lifelong identity.
Families may:
flexi-school in primary but choose full-time school in secondary;
flexi-school during a health recovery;
use flexi-schooling while waiting for ALN evidence or assessment;
use it as part of a careful return after illness, anxiety, bullying or burnout;
return to full-time school when the child is ready.
Because the child remains on roll, returning to full-time schooling does not require a fresh school admission process.
Flexibility is the point of flexi-schooling.

When flexi-schooling is not appropriate
Flexi-schooling is not the right route in every situation.
Example 1: Child unable to attend school at all
If a child cannot attend school at all, this is unlikely to be flexi-schooling.
The issue may be medical tuition, EOTAS, urgent review, safeguarding, health input or alternative provision.
Example 2: School initiating flexi-schooling
If the school is telling the parent to educate at home part-time, that is not genuine parent-requested flexi-schooling.
Parents should ask for the school’s position in writing.
Example 3: Unsafe or traumatising school environment
Flexi-schooling should not be used to hide bullying, safeguarding concerns, discrimination, unmet ALN or a harmful environment.
The correct route may be an urgent IDP review, safeguarding escalation, local authority involvement or alternative placement discussion.
Example 4: Year 11 without structured support
Flexi-schooling in Year 11 can create exam and qualification risks if there is no clear plan.
Families need to think carefully about curriculum coverage, revision, exam entries, coursework, access arrangements and support.
How flexi-schooling can benefit ALN children
For some ALN children, flexi-schooling may:
support pacing;
reduce burnout;
protect emotional wellbeing;
improve the quality of attendance;
allow therapy or medical routines;
improve learning consistency;
reduce sensory overload;
help a child stay connected to school while support is reviewed.
It should not be treated as a replacement for ALP.
It should be part of a clear plan.
How to request flexi-schooling
To persuade a headteacher, your request should:
explain how flexi-schooling helps your child access education;
link the request to your child’s needs;
explain any health, ALN, sensory or emotional factors;
provide structure and reassurance;
clarify safeguarding arrangements;
explain what home learning will look like;
show how the child will continue learning;
ask for attendance coding and review dates to be confirmed.
What usually does not work:
vague requests;
confrontational wording;
framing flexi-schooling as avoidance;
asking the school to fix everything without explaining the proposed arrangement.
Schools respond better to structured, calm, evidence-backed requests.
How schools should respond
Headteachers should:
consider the request reasonably;
respond in writing;
decide based on the child’s needs and the proposed arrangement;
provide a clear attendance plan if agreed;
confirm review dates;
consider whether the IDP needs to be reviewed or updated;
make sure safeguarding arrangements are clear.
They should not:
pressure a parent to deregister;
misstate the law;
refuse for blanket policy reasons without considering the individual child;
use flexi-schooling to avoid ALN support.
Attendance coding
Attendance coding matters.
It can affect safeguarding, attendance records, local authority involvement and how the arrangement is understood later.
Welsh Government attendance material has referred to Code C for agreed flexi-schooling where the school has agreed the pupil is receiving part of their education at home [3].
Parents should ask the school to confirm:
which attendance code will be used;
that agreed home-learning time will not be recorded as unauthorised absence;
who will monitor attendance and engagement;
when the arrangement will be reviewed.
A useful line to include in a request is:
> Please confirm in writing how agreed home-learning sessions will be recorded in the attendance register.
Flexi-schooling and ALN / IDPs
Flexi-schooling does not reduce any ALN duty.
If a child has an IDP, the plan should still be clear about:
the child’s ALN;
the ALP required;
who will deliver the ALP;
where it will be delivered;
how often it will happen;
whether any ALP is expected to happen at home;
whether the parent can realistically support the home element;
how progress will be reviewed.
Schools should:
continue IDP provision;
evidence progress;
review regularly;
include flexi-schooling in the IDP where appropriate;
involve the local authority where needs are complex or responsibility may need to change.
Local authority oversight remains important for complex needs.
For a plain-English explanation of ALP, see What Is Additional Learning Provision (ALP) in Wales?.
Myths and incorrect claims
“Flexi-schooling is illegal.”  
False.
“We do not allow it here.”  
A school may decide whether to agree, but it should still consider the individual request.
“It harms attendance.”  
An agreed arrangement should be authorised and recorded appropriately.
“ALN pupils cannot flexi-school.”  
False. The real question is whether the arrangement meets the individual child’s needs and how ALP will be delivered.
“Safeguarding prevents it.”  
Not automatically. Safeguarding needs to be planned and recorded.
“It must be temporary.”  
Not always. It can be reviewed and changed over time.
“It removes IDP support.”  
False. Flexi-schooling does not remove ALN duties.
How to escalate an unreasonable refusal
If a school refuses flexi-schooling, that does not automatically mean the school has acted unlawfully.
Headteachers have discretion.
But if the refusal is unreasonable, based on incorrect information, or linked to unmet ALN, parents may need to escalate.
Possible routes include:
asking for written reasons;
asking for a meeting with the headteacher;
writing to the chair of governors;
contacting the local authority inclusion or ALN team;
asking for an IDP review where ALN is involved;
using the school complaints process if appropriate;
seeking advice where ALN rights may be affected.
Where the issue is really unmet ALN, unsafe attendance, EOTAS, bullying, disability discrimination or failure to provide ALP, flexi-schooling may not be the main route.
The right route may be an ALN review, local authority involvement, complaint, advocacy or Tribunal advice.
Letter templates
Formal flexi-schooling request
```text
Dear Headteacher,

I am writing to formally request a flexi-schooling arrangement for my child.

This is a parent-requested arrangement. My child would remain on the school roll and attend school for part of the week, while I would provide education at home for the agreed home-learning sessions.

I believe flexi-schooling would support my child’s access to education because:

[briefly explain the child’s needs, health, ALN, sensory needs, fatigue, anxiety, recovery, or other relevant factors]

I propose the following pattern:

Days or sessions in school:
[insert details]

Days or sessions learning at home:
[insert details]

During home-learning time, I would provide structured education including:

[insert outline]

Please could we meet to discuss whether this arrangement can be agreed, including attendance coding, safeguarding arrangements, IDP considerations and review dates.

Please confirm your decision in writing.

Kind regards,

[Name]
```
Informal version
```text
Hi,

I would like to explore a flexi-schooling arrangement for my child.

This would be a parent-requested arrangement. My child would stay on roll and attend school part-time, while I would provide education at home for the agreed home-learning sessions.

I believe this may support my child’s needs because:

[briefly explain]

I am happy to discuss days, structure, attendance coding, safeguarding and review dates.

Thanks,

[Name]
```
Follow-up after refusal or delay
```text
Dear Headteacher,

Thank you for your response about flexi-schooling.

Please could you confirm the reasons for refusal in writing, including whether the decision was based on my child’s individual circumstances and the proposed arrangement.

I would also be grateful if you could confirm whether the school has considered:

- my child’s ALN or possible ALN;
- any health or attendance concerns;
- whether the IDP needs to be reviewed;
- whether any alternative support is needed;
- whether a phased or reviewed arrangement could be considered.

Please can we meet to discuss how my child’s access to education will be supported.

Kind regards,

[Name]
```
Attendance coding clarification letter
```text
Dear Headteacher,

Thank you for confirming the flexi-schooling arrangement.

Please could you confirm in writing how agreed home-learning sessions will be recorded in the attendance register.

Please also confirm that agreed home-learning sessions will not be recorded as unauthorised absence.

Kind regards,

[Name]
```
