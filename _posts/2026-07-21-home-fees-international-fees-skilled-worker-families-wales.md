Over the past few weeks, Learn Without Limits CIC has heard several versions of the same parent question.

The details vary.

The pattern does not.

A family has lived in Wales for years.

A young person has gone through school or college here.

They have worked hard.

They may have received university offers.

Then the family discovers that the young person may be treated as an international or overseas student for university fees.

For many families, that makes university financially impossible.

This issue can affect migrant families in Wales, including families where a parent works in health, social care or another skilled worker route.

Many of these parents are working legally, paying tax, raising children in Welsh communities, and supporting public services.

But their children may still reach university age before the family fits a fee-status category that allows home fees or student finance.

This article explains the issue in plain English.

It is general information, not legal advice, immigration advice, student finance advice, discrimination advice or university admissions advice.

## Who this article is for

This article may be useful if your family lives in Wales and your young person is hoping to go to university, but your family does not yet have British citizenship, Irish citizenship, settled status, indefinite leave to remain, refugee status, humanitarian protection or another status that clearly fits a home-fee or student finance category.

It may be especially relevant where a parent is working in Wales on a Skilled Worker visa, Health and Care Worker visa, or another lawful work route, and the young person is a dependant.

It may also be relevant where a young person has a complex family, care, migration, refugee, asylum, private-life, family-life or mixed-status background.

Every family’s situation is different.

This article does not tell you whether your young person qualifies.

It explains why you should check early and where to get advice.

## Why this article is anonymised

This article is based on repeated themes raised with Learn Without Limits CIC by parents in Wales.

It does not describe one identifiable family.

We have removed names, locations, phone numbers, employers, immigration timelines, schools, colleges, universities and any details that could identify a parent or young person.

We are doing this because families should be able to understand the issue without being exposed to personal risk, stigma or targeting.

The current public climate around migration and race means organisations have a duty to be careful.

The issue matters.

Families do not need to be named for the issue to be understood.

## Why intersectionality matters here

Learn Without Limits CIC works with families whose lives do not fit neatly into one policy box.

A young person may be disabled, have ALN, be racialised, British, migrant, a child of a skilled worker, a child of a refugee family, home educated, in care, a young carer, or part of a faith or linguistic community.

Sometimes several of those things are true at once.

That matters because barriers do not always arrive one at a time.

This article focuses mainly on families where immigration status may affect home fees or student finance.

But we also recognise that some British citizens and settled families may experience problems when unconscious bias leads officials to make incorrect assumptions about immigration status, entitlement, family background or belonging.

A young person should not have to prove they belong because an adult has made an assumption based on their name, accent, ethnicity, faith, family history or community.

Where families are entitled to support, they should receive it.

Where a family’s status creates a genuine fee-status issue, they should be told clearly and early enough to plan.

Where assumptions are wrong, they should be challenged.

## Quick answer

Living in Wales for three years does not automatically mean a young person will pay home university fees.

Going to school or college in Wales does not automatically mean a young person will pay home university fees.

Getting a university offer does not automatically mean the course will be affordable.

Fee status depends on detailed rules.

Those rules can include ordinary residence, immigration status, settled status, protection status, family status, and specific categories set in Welsh regulations.

Universities assess fee status for tuition fees.

Student Finance Wales assesses eligibility for student support.

Families may need to check both.

## Do not assume

Do not assume that three years living in Wales automatically means home fees.

Do not assume that attending school or college in Wales automatically means home fees.

Do not assume that a university offer means the course will be affordable.

Do not assume that Student Finance Wales eligibility and university fee status have both been checked unless the family has written confirmation.

Do not assume that being a dependant on a Skilled Worker visa automatically qualifies a young person for home fees.

Do not assume that a young person has no options if full-time university is not affordable right now.

The safest approach is to check early.

## Check fee status early, ideally in Year 11

Learn Without Limits CIC strongly advises families to check possible higher education fee status by Year 11, or around age 16.

Do not wait until university offers have been made.

This matters because some young people may have lived in Wales for years, attended school or college here, and still be assessed as overseas or international fee payers for university.

This should ideally happen before the young person chooses or commits to post-16 courses, because A level, vocational, apprenticeship, FE and work-linked routes may lead to different higher education options later.

If families know the risk early, they may have more time to:

- ask universities how fee status is assessed;
- check the Welsh fee-status categories;
- ask Student Finance Wales about student support eligibility;
- understand whether immigration status may affect future eligibility;
- speak to Careers Wales about alternative routes into higher education;
- plan FE, apprenticeships, employment-linked training or part-time routes;
- consider whether delaying university may be financially safer;
- get regulated immigration advice where immigration status is part of the issue.

By Year 13, some options may already have narrowed.

The young person may already have chosen subjects, applied through UCAS, received offers and emotionally committed to a route.

Finding out late can be devastating.

Finding out earlier gives families more room to plan.

For more on post-16 planning, see [Post-16 Educational Provision in Wales for ALN Young People](https://blog.learnwithoutlimitscic.org/2026/07/post-16-educational-provision-wales-aln-young-people.html).

## Timing can matter

Families should ask about timing, not only status.

Some fee-status and student-finance rules look at the young person’s position on the first day of the first academic year of the course.

That means families should not assume that a later change in immigration status will automatically fix the fee position for a course that has already started.

Ask the university and Student Finance Wales how timing applies to your young person’s situation.

## Why this matters for health and social care worker families

Many families in Wales have moved here through lawful work routes.

Some parents work in the NHS.

Some work in care homes, domiciliary care, clinical support, social care or other public-service roles.

They may be doing work Wales needs.

But lawful residence and employment do not automatically mean a young person qualifies for home university fees or student finance.

This can feel deeply unfair to families.

A parent may have been recruited into a shortage sector, worked hard, paid tax, paid rent, raised children locally, and built a life in Wales.

Their child may then reach university age and find that the door to higher education is open on paper but closed in practice because of international fees.

That is not only a university finance issue.

It is a post-16 progression issue.

It is also a workforce retention issue for Wales.

If families only find out when the young person is ready to start university, the family has very little time to respond.

## Why three years in Wales may not be enough

Many parents hear “three years” and assume that means:

> We have lived here for three years, so my child should get home fees.

The reality is more technical.

UKCISA explains that there are many categories of student who can qualify for home fees for higher education in Wales [1].

UKCISA also warns that students need to meet all the requirements for a category, and that technical words in the regulations may not mean the same thing as they do in ordinary life [1].

That is important.

A family may be ordinarily living in Wales in a normal, everyday sense.

But fee-status rules may still ask different questions.

Those questions may include:

- What immigration status does the young person have?
- What immigration status does the parent have?
- Is the young person settled in the UK?
- Has the young person been ordinarily resident for the relevant period?
- Was the main purpose of residence full-time education?
- Does the young person fit a special-status category?
- Does a family-member category apply?
- Does refugee status, humanitarian protection, family life, private life or another category apply?
- Does the young person meet Student Finance Wales criteria separately?

This is why families should not rely only on ordinary language.

They need the actual category.

## Home fees and international fees are not a small difference

Home-fee students are charged under fee rules and caps that apply to eligible home students.

International or overseas students are usually charged a different fee rate.

International fees vary by university and course.

They can be far higher than home fees.

For many working families, especially health and social care families, international fees are not realistic.

That means a young person may receive a university offer and still be unable to take up the place.

The issue is not aspiration.

It is affordability.

## Fee status and student finance are linked, but not identical

Families may need to check two things.

First, the university’s fee-status decision.

This decides whether the young person is charged home fees or international fees.

Second, Student Finance Wales eligibility.

This decides whether the young person can receive support such as tuition fee loans, maintenance support or other student finance.

Student Finance Wales says students who cannot identify as either a tuition-fee-and-living-costs student or a tuition-fee-only funding student may not be eligible for Student Finance Wales funding [2].

A family should not assume that one decision automatically proves the other.

Ask both.

Get answers in writing.

## Why Skilled Worker dependants can fall into a gap

A dependant on a Skilled Worker visa may be lawfully living in Wales.

They may have attended school or college here.

They may feel Welsh.

They may have friends, teachers, community ties and a future here.

But that does not automatically mean they fit a home-fee or student finance category.

The issue is not whether the family belongs in everyday life.

The issue is whether the young person fits the technical rules used for higher education fees and student finance.

That is why this problem can come as such a shock.

Families may not realise there is a higher education fee-status risk until university offers have already been made.

## What families should ask the university

Each university should have a fee-status assessment process.

Families can ask:

- Will my young person be assessed as a home or international fee payer?
- What fee-status category is being considered?
- Has the young person been assessed against all potentially relevant Wales categories?
- What evidence is needed?
- Can the university give the decision in writing?
- If the young person is assessed as international, what reason is being given?
- Is there a reassessment or review process?
- What happens if immigration status changes before the course starts?
- What happens if immigration status changes after the course starts?
- Does the university offer any scholarships, bursaries or hardship routes for students in this situation?
- Who in the university can explain the fee-status decision?

Keep copies of everything.

If a decision is given by phone, ask for it in writing.

## Email template: asking a university to assess fee status

Subject: Request for fee-status assessment before application or enrolment

Dear [University Fee Status Team],

I am writing to ask how my young person’s fee status would be assessed for undergraduate study in Wales.

They currently live in Wales and are considering higher education. Our family immigration status may affect whether they are assessed as a home or international fee payer.

Please can you confirm:

- what evidence you would need;
- which Wales fee-status categories may be relevant;
- whether you can provide a written fee-status assessment;
- how reassessment works if immigration status changes before the course starts;
- what review or appeal process is available if we disagree with the decision.

Thank you.

## What families should ask Student Finance Wales

Student Finance Wales decides student support eligibility.

Families can ask:

- Can my young person apply for full undergraduate student finance?
- If not, can they apply for tuition-fee-only funding?
- Which nationality or residency category is being considered?
- What evidence is needed?
- Does immigration status affect eligibility?
- Does the young person need settled status, leave under a specific category, or another qualifying route?
- What happens if status changes before the course starts?
- What happens if status changes during the course?
- Are part-time routes assessed differently?
- Are there healthcare, social work or other professional-course rules that may be relevant?

Again, ask for written confirmation.

## Evidence families may need to gather

Families should not wait until the last minute to find documents.

The evidence needed will depend on the young person’s situation, but it may include:

- passport details;
- visa or eVisa evidence;
- biometric residence permit evidence, where relevant;
- Home Office letters;
- share codes;
- proof of date of entry to the UK;
- proof of residence in Wales;
- school or college attendance evidence;
- offer letters;
- UCAS correspondence;
- proof of parent or family immigration status;
- proof of relationship to the parent;
- evidence of settled status, indefinite leave, refugee status, humanitarian protection, family life, private life or another relevant status, where applicable;
- Student Finance Wales correspondence;
- university fee-status correspondence.

Do not send original documents unless the organisation clearly asks for them and explains how they will be handled.

Keep copies.

Keep dates.

Keep emails.

## When immigration advice may be needed

Sometimes the fee-status question cannot be separated from immigration status.

Families may need advice about:

- whether a young person may become eligible later;
- whether a parent’s future immigration route affects the child;
- whether settlement may change the position;
- whether another immigration category applies;
- whether there are risks in making assumptions;
- whether advice is needed before taking action.

Learn Without Limits CIC cannot give immigration advice.

Immigration advice should come from a regulated immigration adviser or solicitor.

Families should be cautious about taking immigration advice from WhatsApp groups, community rumours, social media posts or people who are not regulated.

Community knowledge can be helpful because it may help families spot the issue.

But immigration advice can carry serious consequences if it is wrong.

## Careers Wales should be involved early

If a family discovers a fee-status risk in Year 11 or Year 12, Careers Wales may be able to help the young person think through alternative routes.

That might include:

- FE college;
- access routes;
- apprenticeships;
- degree apprenticeships where available;
- employment-linked training;
- part-time study;
- online or distance learning;
- delaying higher education until the family position changes;
- building work experience and qualifications first;
- exploring different funding routes.

Careers Wales has professionally qualified advisers and says its services are free, impartial and confidential.

Home-educating families should contact Careers Wales directly.

Do not assume a school, college or local authority will automatically make the introduction.

Careers Wales says it has a dedicated team of Careers Advisers who work with home-educated young people, and families can contact them for information or appointments [5].

Parents can use:

- [Careers Wales: Contact Us](https://careerswales.gov.wales/contact-us)
- [Careers Wales: Our Centres](https://careerswales.gov.wales/contact-us/our-centres)
- [Careers Wales: Your home-educated child](https://careerswales.gov.wales/parents/your-home-educated-child)
- [Careers Wales: Wales' post 16 education and training provision](https://careerswales.gov.wales/courses-and-training/wales-post-16-education-and-training-provision)
- [Careers Wales: What is an Apprenticeship?](https://careerswales.gov.wales/apprenticeships/what-is-an-apprenticeship)

For more on route planning, see [ALN Transitions in Wales: Nursery, School, College and Beyond](https://blog.learnwithoutlimitscic.org/2026/07/aln-transitions-wales-nursery-school-college.html).

## What if the standard full-time university route is closed for now?

A young person may still have options.

Those options may not be perfect.

They may not be the route the young person hoped for.

But early planning can prevent a complete cliff edge.

Families can explore:

- staying in FE longer to build qualifications;
- HNC, HND or foundation degree routes;
- apprenticeships;
- degree apprenticeships where available;
- employer-supported training;
- part-time study;
- online or distance learning;
- Open University-style routes;
- scholarships and bursaries;
- delaying university until eligibility changes;
- work experience followed by later study.

Families should not assume the young person has failed because university is not affordable at 18.

The problem may be the funding route, not the young person.

For more on preventing young people from drifting without a route, see [Disabled Young Adults After 19: Wales Needs to Treat NEET Risk as a Prevention Crisis](https://blog.learnwithoutlimitscic.org/2026/07/disabled-young-adults-after-19-neet-prevention-wales.html).

## Why this is a Wales progression issue

This issue sits inside a bigger post-16 picture.

Wales needs young people to progress into higher education, skilled work, health care, social care, teaching, engineering, technology and public service.

Some migrant families are already contributing to those sectors.

Their children may be exactly the young people Wales should want to keep, educate and support into skilled futures.

If those young people are blocked by fee-status barriers that families only discover late, Wales risks wasting talent.

That matters for:

- families;
- young people;
- universities;
- colleges;
- employers;
- health and social care workforce planning;
- racial equity;
- community cohesion;
- NEET prevention.

A young person can be living in Wales, learning in Wales and contributing to Wales, but still find that the higher education system treats them as financially outside.

Families need to know this early enough to plan.

At a policy level, families recruited into public-service roles should be told about this risk early, before their children reach university application age.

## Where families can get advice

Fee status and student finance can be confusing because different organisations answer different parts of the question.

Families may need more than one source of advice.

### The university fee-status team

Each university should be able to explain how it has assessed the young person’s fee status.

Ask for the decision in writing.

Ask what evidence was considered.

Ask what review or reassessment process exists.

### UKCISA

UKCISA publishes detailed fee-status guidance for Wales.

It is a key starting point for understanding the categories that may qualify a student for home fees.

UKCISA does not award funding, set the law or override a university decision.

Use:

- [UKCISA: Full list of categories for HE in Wales](https://www.ukcisa.org.uk/student-advice/fees/full-list-of-categories-for-he-in-wales/)

### Student Finance Wales

Student Finance Wales assesses eligibility for student support.

A young person may need to check both university fee status and Student Finance Wales eligibility.

Use:

- [Student Finance Wales: Full-time undergraduate students](https://www.studentfinancewales.co.uk/undergraduate-finance/full-time/)
- [Student Finance Wales: Who qualifies](https://www.studentfinancewales.co.uk/undergraduate-finance/full-time/tuition-fee-and-living-cost-students/who-qualifies/)
- [Student Finance Wales: Tuition fee only funding, who qualifies](https://www.studentfinancewales.co.uk/undergraduate-finance/full-time/tuition-fee-only-funding-student/who-qualifies/)

### Careers Wales

Careers Wales can help young people and families explore post-16 and post-18 routes.

This is especially important if full-time university is not affordable right now.

Use:

- [Careers Wales: Contact Us](https://careerswales.gov.wales/contact-us)
- [Careers Wales: Our Centres](https://careerswales.gov.wales/contact-us/our-centres)
- [Careers Wales: Post-16 education and training provision](https://careerswales.gov.wales/courses-and-training/wales-post-16-education-and-training-provision)
- [Careers Wales: What is an Apprenticeship?](https://careerswales.gov.wales/apprenticeships/what-is-an-apprenticeship)
- [Careers Wales: Your home-educated child](https://careerswales.gov.wales/parents/your-home-educated-child)

### Citizens Advice

Citizens Advice can help families understand where to get immigration advice and may have specialist immigration advisers in some areas.

Use:

- [Citizens Advice: Get specialist immigration advice](https://www.citizensadvice.org.uk/wales/immigration/get-help/get-immigration-advice/)

### Regulated immigration advisers or solicitors

Where immigration status, settlement, visa conditions or future eligibility may affect fee status, families should use a regulated immigration adviser or solicitor.

The Immigration Advice Authority regulates immigration advisers in the UK.

GOV.UK has an adviser finder.

Use:

- [GOV.UK: Information for advice seekers](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/information-for-advice-seekers)
- [GOV.UK: Find an immigration adviser](https://www.gov.uk/find-an-immigration-adviser)

### Welsh Refugee Council

Families with refugee status, asylum-related questions, humanitarian protection or forced migration backgrounds may need specialist refugee or asylum support.

Welsh Refugee Council says it can provide advice and support about educational opportunities including further education, higher education, vocational training and online learning [12].

Use:

- [Welsh Refugee Council: Help with Education](https://wrc.wales/help-with-education/)

### EYST Wales

EYST Wales provides support for ethnic minority people in Wales.

Its Right to Education project is described as an all-Wales specialist educational, advocacy and support service targeted at ethnic minority young people aged 11 to 19 [13].

This may be useful for families needing education advocacy or signposting, although families should still use specialist fee-status, student finance or regulated immigration advice where needed.

Use:

- [EYST Wales: Right to Education](https://www.eyst.org.uk/right-to-education)
- [EYST Wales: What We Offer](https://www.eyst.org.uk/what-we-offer-2)

## What parents can do now

If your young person is in Year 11, Year 12 or Year 13 and your family has immigration status that may affect higher education funding, consider taking these steps:

1. Ask the young person what route they are hoping for.
2. Contact Careers Wales.
3. Check UKCISA’s Wales fee-status categories.
4. Ask universities how fee status is assessed.
5. Ask Student Finance Wales about support eligibility.
6. Gather immigration and residence evidence.
7. Get advice before making assumptions.
8. Keep written records.
9. Plan an affordable route, not just an ideal route.
10. Recheck if immigration status changes.

This does not solve the unfairness.

But it may prevent a young person from reaching university offer stage with no affordable plan.

## What Learn Without Limits CIC thinks

Families should not be finding this out by accident.

If a young person in Wales may be treated as an international fee payer, families need to know early enough to plan.

That is especially true for children of health and social care workers, skilled worker families, refugee families, racialised migrant families, home-educated young people and young people already at risk of fragile post-16 progression.

This is not only a university finance issue.

It is a navigation issue.

The standard system often assumes families know which questions to ask.

Many do not.

They may not know that “living here for three years” is not the same as qualifying for home fees.

They may not know that fee status and student finance are separate checks.

They may not know that immigration status can affect higher education routes.

They may not know that Careers Wales can help them plan alternatives before the crisis point.

This includes challenging barriers created by immigration status rules, poor signposting, late information, and incorrect assumptions about families who are British, settled, racialised, migrant or mixed-status.

Good guidance should not wait until the young person is holding an offer they cannot afford to accept.

Wales needs young people to progress.

That means families need clear information early enough to use it.
