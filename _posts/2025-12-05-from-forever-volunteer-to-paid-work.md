<h1>From “Forever Volunteer” to Paid Work: How Volunteering Should Be a Bridge, Not a Dead End</h1>

<p class="lead">
Volunteering should build confidence, evidence, skills, references and progression. It should not become a permanent unpaid holding pattern.
</p>

<p>
This article is part of Learn Without Limits CIC’s Bridge and progression cluster. Bridge is about creating safe stepping stones back towards learning, training, employment or wider participation when the jump from crisis to formal progression is too large.
</p>

<h2>Understanding the “forever volunteer” problem</h2>

<p>
Many disabled and neurodivergent young people in Wales want to contribute, learn and build confidence. Volunteering can be a positive route into experience, routine and community connection. But without clear boundaries, progression goals or recognition, it can quietly become a long-term unpaid holding pattern.
</p>

<p>
That is the “forever volunteer” problem: when someone gives time, skill and commitment, but the role never leads to a reference, training, a certificate, a paid opportunity, a college application example, or a clearer next step.
</p>

<p>
One public example is the case of Tom Boyd, an autistic man who had volunteered for years at a supermarket before his family asked whether he could be considered for paid work. The case attracted national attention because it raised a wider question: when does “opportunity” become unpaid labour without progression?
</p>

<p>
<a href="https://www.theguardian.com/society/2025/oct/23/asda-hires-autistic-man-who-was-let-go-by-waitrose-after-years-of-volunteering" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Read the Guardian report on Tom Boyd’s case</a>.
</p>

<p>
Tom’s story is not proof that every volunteering role is exploitative. Many organisations support volunteers well. But it does show why disabled people, neurodivergent people and parent carers need to ask what a volunteering role is actually building towards.
</p>

<h2>Why this can happen</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Funding pressure:</strong> some organisations rely heavily on unpaid labour because they do not have enough paid capacity.</li>
  <li><strong>Low expectations:</strong> disabled volunteers may be treated as “helping out” rather than as people with skills, ambitions and progression goals.</li>
  <li><strong>Blurred boundaries:</strong> a role may begin as supported volunteering but slowly become regular unpaid work.</li>
  <li><strong>Weak progression planning:</strong> no one agrees what the volunteer should gain from the experience.</li>
  <li><strong>System gaps:</strong> employability, benefits, education, support and volunteering routes often do not join up cleanly.</li>
</ul>

<p>
Unless volunteering is structured around the volunteer’s own goals, it can become a permanent loop instead of a bridge.
</p>

<h2>When volunteering is done well</h2>

<p>
Volunteering can be valuable when it supports the person volunteering, not only the organisation receiving their labour.
</p>

<p>
Welsh Government’s Third Sector Scheme recognises the role of third sector organisations and community groups in supporting communities, participation and civic life in Wales.
</p>

<p>
<a href="https://www.gov.wales/third-sector-scheme-supporting-communities-and-volunteers-html" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Read the Welsh Government Third Sector Scheme</a>.
</p>

<p>
Good volunteering can help someone:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>practise communication, teamwork, timekeeping and reliability;</li>
  <li>build a CV with real examples;</li>
  <li>gain current experience after time away from school, college or work;</li>
  <li>try out a field before committing to a course or job;</li>
  <li>meet people who may later provide a reference;</li>
  <li>build confidence after exclusion, illness, burnout or caring responsibilities;</li>
  <li>create evidence for college, training, supported employment or paid work.</li>
</ul>

<p>
One older TimeBank and Reed.co.uk employer survey, cited by UWE Bristol, reported that 73% of employers would employ a candidate with volunteer experience over someone without it, and that 94% believed volunteering adds to skills.
</p>

<p>
<a href="https://www.uve.ac.uk/life/activities/volunteering/reasons-to-volunteer" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Read UWE Bristol’s summary of the TimeBank and Reed.co.uk survey</a>.
</p>

<p>
The point is not that volunteering guarantees paid work. It does not. The point is that volunteering should create usable evidence of skill, reliability, confidence and contribution.
</p>

<h2>What good volunteering should give you</h2>

<p>
A useful volunteering role should offer at least some of the following:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>a named contact or supervisor;</li>
  <li>a clear role description;</li>
  <li>agreed tasks and boundaries;</li>
  <li>training or induction where needed;</li>
  <li>a record of what you have done;</li>
  <li>feedback on your contribution;</li>
  <li>a reference after a reasonable period;</li>
  <li>examples you can use in a CV, college application or interview;</li>
  <li>a review point to discuss next steps.</li>
</ul>

<p>
If a role gives the organisation regular labour but gives the volunteer no learning, no evidence, no recognition and no route forward, it is reasonable to question whether that role is fair.
</p>

<h2>For parent carers returning to work</h2>

<p>
The same pattern can affect parent carers returning after years outside paid work. A volunteering role can be a useful bridge back into confidence, routine, references and current experience. But it should still have boundaries, agreed tasks and a clear sense of what the volunteer is gaining from the arrangement.
</p>

<p>
Parent carers are often highly skilled. They may have managed education processes, health appointments, forms, meetings, advocacy, logistics, household budgets, caring routines and crisis problem-solving for years. The issue is not usually lack of ability. It is often lack of recent, recognised evidence.
</p>

<p>
A good volunteering role can help turn hidden labour into visible evidence. A poor one can simply add another unpaid responsibility to an already overloaded life.
</p>

</p>

<h2>The volunteer-to-work toolkit</h2>

<p>
Volunteering should serve you, not just the organisation. These steps can help you stay in control.
</p>

<h3>1. Choose opportunities that fit your goals</h3>

<p>
Start with what you want the role to help you practise or evidence.
</p>

<ul>
  <li>If you want retail experience, look for customer-facing or stock roles.</li>
  <li>If you like fixing things, look for a repair café, maker space or upcycling project.</li>
  <li>If you are creative, look for arts, media, craft, social media or community event roles.</li>
  <li>If you want admin experience, ask about newsletters, spreadsheets, booking systems or basic office tasks.</li>
  <li>If you need confidence first, look for low-pressure roles with predictable tasks.</li>
</ul>

<p>
You do not need a perfect long-term plan. You just need a reason for choosing the role.
</p>

<h3>2. Ask what the role includes</h3>

<p>
Before you start, ask:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>What tasks would I be doing?</li>
  <li>How many hours are expected?</li>
  <li>Who would support me?</li>
  <li>Is there training or induction?</li>
  <li>Can I get a record of tasks completed?</li>
  <li>Can I ask for a reference after a set period?</li>
  <li>Is there any route into paid work, training or a different responsibility?</li>
</ul>

<p>
A good organisation should not be offended by these questions. They are normal questions about boundaries and expectations.
</p>

<h3>3. Make first contact, even if you have never volunteered before</h3>

<p>
You can ask someone to help you reach out. This could be:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>an ALN support worker;</li>
  <li>a youth worker;</li>
  <li>a parent, carer, sibling or personal assistant;</li>
  <li>a Careers Wales adviser;</li>
  <li>a college support worker;</li>
  <li>a Jobcentre Disability Employment Adviser;</li>
  <li>a trusted community worker.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Email template:</strong></p>

<blockquote>
  <p><strong>Subject:</strong> Volunteering enquiry</p>

  <p>Dear [Organisation Name],</p>

  <p>I am interested in volunteering and would like to get involved in something meaningful. I am especially interested in [brief interest, for example animals, history, events, admin, digital work, repairs or community support].</p>

  <p>I have [briefly say any relevant experience, or say you are new to volunteering]. I am hoping to build confidence, skills and experience.</p>

  <p>Could you let me know whether you have any suitable opportunities, and what support or induction you provide for volunteers?</p>

  <p>Many thanks,<br>
  [Your First Name]</p>
</blockquote>

<p>
Not every organisation replies. That does not mean you have done anything wrong. Try again, contact a different organisation, or ask someone to follow up with you.
</p>

<h3>4. Set boundaries and track progress</h3>

<p>
To avoid getting stuck in a loop, use simple SMART targets. SMART means specific, measurable, achievable, relevant and time-bound.
</p>

<p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Volunteer once a week for three months, then ask for a reference.</li>
  <li>Learn three new tasks that can go on my CV.</li>
  <li>Help with one public event and record what I contributed.</li>
  <li>Complete a short training session or induction.</li>
  <li>Ask for a progress conversation after six weeks.</li>
</ul>

<p>
If a placement is part of a college, Jobcentre, supported employment or publicly funded scheme, it is reasonable to ask what progress is expected and how it will be reviewed.
</p>

<p><strong>Examples of useful asks:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>“By week six, can we review what I have learned?”</li>
  <li>“Can you confirm whether this role offers a route into paid work or training?”</li>
  <li>“Can I have a written record of the tasks I have completed?”</li>
  <li>“Can we agree what I need to do to receive a reference?”</li>
  <li>“Can you help me identify one next step after this role?”</li>
</ul>

<p>
Write down what is agreed. This helps if plans drift or if the role starts taking more from you than it gives back.
</p>

<h3>5. Use scripts to take back control</h3>

<p>
Feeling stuck? Try these phrases:
</p>

<blockquote>
  <p>“I have been doing the same tasks for a while. Could we talk about a next step or a different responsibility?”</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote>
  <p>“I would like to understand how this role could help me move towards paid work, college, training or another progression route. Could we plan that together?”</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote>
  <p>“Would you be willing to write me a reference or help me record what I have learned so far?”</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote>
  <p>“I am happy to contribute, but I need the hours to stay within what we agreed.”</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote>
  <p>“Can we review whether this role is still helping me meet my goals?”</p>
</blockquote>

</p>

</blockquote>

<h2>Volunteering is not always about paid work, and that is okay</h2>

<p>
Not everyone volunteers because they want a job. Some people volunteer for:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>stability;</li>
  <li>routine;</li>
  <li>social contact;</li>
  <li>confidence;</li>
  <li>wellbeing;</li>
  <li>community connection;</li>
  <li>recovery after illness, burnout or crisis;</li>
  <li>a sense of purpose.</li>
</ul>

<p>
Those are valid reasons. The key issue is whether the experience supports you emotionally, practically and on your own terms.
</p>

<p>
A role does not have to lead to paid work to be worthwhile. But it should not quietly trap you in unpaid labour that benefits everyone except you.
</p>

<h2>What Learn Without Limits means by Bridge</h2>

<p>
At Learn Without Limits CIC, Bridge means a stepping stone, not a dead end.
</p>

<p>
For young people, Bridge can mean rebuilding confidence, producing real examples of work, gaining experience, testing interests and creating evidence for college, training or future employment.
</p>

<p>
For parent carers, Bridge can mean rebuilding confidence after a career break, turning hidden skills into visible evidence, gaining recent examples for a CV, and moving back towards paid work where that is the right goal.
</p>

<p>
A good Bridge role should be bounded, useful, supervised and honest about what it can and cannot offer.
</p>

<p>
That is why volunteering should never be used as a substitute for paid staff where safeguarding, technical governance, casework, statutory duties or organisational accountability are required. Those responsibilities need proper paid capacity and governance.
</p>

<h2>Final thoughts</h2>

<p>
Volunteering can empower, connect and open doors. It can also exploit people and delay progress if it is badly designed.
</p>

<p>
Your time, energy and skills have value. A good volunteering role should recognise that value by helping you build confidence, evidence, references, skills or next steps.
</p>

<p>
The best volunteering is a bridge. It helps someone move from isolation, uncertainty or exclusion towards something more stable.
</p>

<p>
It should never become a dead end.
</p>
