<p>&nbsp;</p><h1 data-end="382" data-start="300">Attendance warnings, fines and prosecution in Wales: what parents need to know</h1><p data-end="441" data-start="383"><em data-end="441" data-start="383">(especially if your child has <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=define+Additional+Learning+Needs&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Additional Learning Needs</a>)</em></p><blockquote data-end="893" data-start="443"><p data-end="893" data-start="445"><strong data-end="469" data-start="445">Important disclaimer</strong><br data-end="472" data-start="469" />Learn Without Limits CIC is not a law firm and does not provide legal advice.<br data-end="554" data-start="551" />This article is for general information and guidance only, based on Welsh and UK legislation, statutory guidance, and publicly reported cases.<br data-end="701" data-start="698" />It should not be relied upon as a substitute for independent legal advice.<br data-end="780" data-start="777" />Parents facing enforcement action, fines, or prosecution may wish to seek specialist legal or advocacy support.</p></blockquote><hr data-end="898" data-start="895" /><h2 data-end="949" data-start="900">1. The legal duty to secure education in Wales</h2><p data-end="1040" data-start="951">The starting point in law is <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=section+7+Education+Act+1996&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">section 7 of the Education Act 1996</a>, which applies in Wales.</p><p data-end="1057" data-start="1042">The Act states:</p><blockquote data-end="1378" data-start="1059"><p data-end="1378" data-start="1061">“The parent of every child of compulsory school age shall cause him to receive <strong data-end="1182" data-start="1140">efficient full-time education suitable</strong><br data-end="1185" data-start="1182" />(a) to his age, ability and aptitude, and<br data-end="1231" data-start="1228" />(b) to any special educational needs he may have,<br data-end="1285" data-start="1282" />either by regular attendance at school or otherwise.”<br data-end="1343" data-start="1340" /><em data-end="1378" data-start="1345">(<a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=Education+Act+1996&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Education Act 1996</a>, section 7)</em></p></blockquote><p data-end="1434" data-start="1380">Two points matter legally and are often misunderstood:</p><ol data-end="1610" data-start="1436"><li data-end="1514" data-start="1436"><p data-end="1514" data-start="1439">The duty is to secure <strong data-end="1483" data-start="1461">suitable education</strong>, not attendance at any cost.</p></li><li data-end="1610" data-start="1515"><p data-end="1610" data-start="1518">Regular school attendance is one lawful way of meeting the duty, but it is not the only way.</p></li></ol><p data-end="1747" data-start="1612">However, this is still a <strong data-end="1654" data-start="1637">parental duty</strong>, and failure to meet it can carry serious legal consequences if the statutory tests are met.</p><hr data-end="1752" data-start="1749" /><h2 data-end="1800" data-start="1754">2. What attendance letters mean in practice</h2><p data-end="1886" data-start="1802">Most attendance letters sent by schools or local authorities are <strong data-end="1885" data-start="1867">administrative</strong>.</p><p data-end="1915" data-start="1888">They are usually issued to:</p><ul data-end="2036" data-start="1916"><li data-end="1957" data-start="1916"><p data-end="1957" data-start="1918">notify parents of attendance concerns</p></li><li data-end="1992" data-start="1958"><p data-end="1992" data-start="1960">request engagement or meetings</p></li><li data-end="2036" data-start="1993"><p data-end="2036" data-start="1995">warn that enforcement action may follow</p></li></ul><p data-end="2052" data-start="2038">At this stage:</p><ul data-end="2155" data-start="2053"><li data-end="2092" data-start="2053"><p data-end="2092" data-start="2055">no criminal offence has been proven</p></li><li data-end="2124" data-start="2093"><p data-end="2124" data-start="2095">no fine has yet been issued</p></li><li data-end="2155" data-start="2125"><p data-end="2155" data-start="2127">no prosecution has started</p></li></ul><p data-end="2335" data-start="2157">That said, attendance letters are important.<br data-end="2204" data-start="2201" />Failure to respond or engage can later be relied upon by a local authority as evidence that a parent did not take reasonable steps.</p><hr data-end="2340" data-start="2337" /><h2 data-end="2397" data-start="2342">3. Fixed Penalty Notices (attendance fines) in Wales</h2><h3 data-end="2418" data-start="2399">The legal basis</h3><p data-end="2519" data-start="2420">Attendance fines in Wales are governed by the <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=Education+%28Penalty+Notices%29+%28Wales%29+Regulations+2013&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Education (Penalty Notices) (Wales) Regulations 2013</a>.</p><p data-end="2624" data-start="2521">These regulations allow a local authority to issue a <strong data-end="2598" data-start="2574"><a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=define+Fixed+Penalty+Notice&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Fixed Penalty Notice</a></strong> for unauthorised absence.</p><p data-end="2668" data-start="2626">Key legal facts parents should understand:</p><ul data-end="3042" data-start="2670"><li data-end="2727" data-start="2670"><p data-end="2727" data-start="2672">Penalty notices are <strong data-end="2709" data-start="2692">discretionary</strong>, not automatic.</p></li><li data-end="2818" data-start="2728"><p data-end="2818" data-start="2730">They are intended as an <strong data-end="2784" data-start="2754">alternative to prosecution</strong>, not a replacement for the law.</p></li><li data-end="2907" data-start="2819"><p data-end="2907" data-start="2821">Paying a penalty notice usually prevents prosecution for the same period of absence.</p></li><li data-end="3042" data-start="2908"><p data-end="3042" data-start="2910">Failure to pay may lead the authority to consider prosecution for the <strong data-end="3013" data-start="2980">underlying attendance offence</strong>, not for non-payment itself.</p></li></ul><p data-end="3094" data-start="3044">A penalty notice is <strong data-end="3071" data-start="3064">not</strong> a criminal conviction.</p><hr data-end="3099" data-start="3096" /><h2 data-end="3147" data-start="3101">4. Welsh Government guidance on enforcement</h2><p data-end="3239" data-start="3149"><a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=Welsh+Government&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Welsh Government</a> guidance makes clear that enforcement should not be the default response.</p><p data-end="3317" data-start="3241">The <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=Attendance+Framework+for+Wales&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Attendance Framework for Wales</a> states that legal intervention should be:</p><blockquote data-end="3454" data-start="3319"><p data-end="3454" data-start="3321">“used sparingly, consistently, and only where other approaches have failed.”<br data-end="3400" data-start="3397" /><em data-end="3454" data-start="3402">(Welsh Government, Attendance Framework for Wales)</em></p></blockquote><p data-end="3609" data-start="3456">The guidance also stresses the importance of understanding <strong data-end="3522" data-start="3515">why</strong> a child is not attending school, rather than relying solely on attendance percentages.</p><p data-end="3715" data-start="3611">Although guidance is not law, local authorities are expected to have regard to it when making decisions.</p><hr data-end="3720" data-start="3717" /><h2 data-end="3759" data-start="3722">5. When fines may be inappropriate</h2><p data-end="3891" data-start="3761">Welsh Government guidance and established practice indicate that penalty notices are often inappropriate where absence relates to:</p><ul data-end="4126" data-start="3893"><li data-end="3928" data-start="3893"><p data-end="3928" data-start="3895"><a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=disability+or+long-term+illness+and+school+attendance&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">disability or long-term illness</a></p></li><li data-end="3961" data-start="3929"><p data-end="3961" data-start="3931">emotional distress or trauma</p></li><li data-end="3997" data-start="3962"><p data-end="3997" data-start="3964"><a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=unmet+Additional+Learning+Needs+and+school+attendance&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">unmet Additional Learning Needs</a></p></li><li data-end="4040" data-start="3998"><p data-end="4040" data-start="4000">unsuitable or unsafe school placements</p></li><li data-end="4126" data-start="4041"><p data-end="4126" data-start="4043">situations where the school itself has said it cannot safely meet a child’s needs</p></li></ul><p data-end="4368" data-start="4128">It is important to be clear that guidance does not create immunity from enforcement.<br data-end="4215" data-start="4212" />These factors do not automatically prevent fines or prosecution, but they are <strong data-end="4312" data-start="4293">highly relevant</strong> to whether enforcement is reasonable and proportionate.</p><hr data-end="4373" data-start="4370" /><h2 data-end="4421" data-start="4375">6. Additional Learning Needs and attendance</h2><p data-end="4618" data-start="4423">Under the <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=Additional+Learning+Needs+and+Education+Tribunal+%28Wales%29+Act+2018&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Additional Learning Needs and Education Tribunal (Wales) Act 2018</a> and the <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=ALN+Code+for+Wales&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">ALN Code for Wales</a>, children with ALN are entitled to <strong data-end="4594" data-start="4561"><a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=define+additional+learning+provision&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">additional learning provision</a></strong> that meets their needs.</p><p data-end="4640" data-start="4620">The ALN Code states:</p><blockquote data-end="4806" data-start="4642"><p data-end="4806" data-start="4644">“A child has additional learning needs if they have a learning difficulty or <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=disability+and+school+attendance&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">disability</a> which calls for additional learning provision.”<br data-end="4782" data-start="4779" /><em data-end="4806" data-start="4784">(ALN Code for Wales)</em></p></blockquote><p data-end="4977" data-start="4808">If required provision is not in place, or the placement cannot meet the child’s needs, non-attendance may reflect <strong data-end="4940" data-start="4922">system failure</strong> rather than wilful parental neglect.</p><p data-end="5104" data-start="4979">This does not remove parental responsibility, but it is directly relevant to how attendance enforcement should be approached.</p><hr data-end="5109" data-start="5106" /><h2 data-end="5153" data-start="5111">7. When schools say a child is “unsafe”</h2><p data-end="5190" data-start="5155">If a school states that a child is:</p><ul data-end="5299" data-start="5191"><li data-end="5219" data-start="5191"><p data-end="5219" data-start="5193">unsafe when dysregulated</p></li><li data-end="5264" data-start="5220"><p data-end="5264" data-start="5222">unable to cope in the school environment</p></li><li data-end="5299" data-start="5265"><p data-end="5299" data-start="5267">a risk to themselves or others</p></li></ul><p data-end="5337" data-start="5301">this raises serious questions about:</p><ul data-end="5459" data-start="5338"><li data-end="5370" data-start="5338"><p data-end="5370" data-start="5340">suitability of the placement</p></li><li data-end="5404" data-start="5371"><p data-end="5404" data-start="5373">safeguarding responsibilities</p></li><li data-end="5459" data-start="5405"><p data-end="5459" data-start="5407">reasonable adjustments under the <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=Equality+Act+2010&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Equality Act 2010</a></p></li></ul><p data-end="5655" data-start="5461">A local authority may still pursue attendance action, but parents are entitled to challenge the fairness and logic of enforcement where the school itself says it cannot safely educate the child.</p><hr data-end="5660" data-start="5657" /><h2 data-end="5714" data-start="5662">8. When non-attendance becomes a criminal offence</h2><h3 data-end="5731" data-start="5716">The offence</h3><p data-end="5815" data-start="5733"><a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=section+444+Education+Act+1996&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">Prosecution for non-attendance occurs under section 444 of the Education Act 1996</a>.</p><p data-end="5848" data-start="5817">A court must be satisfied that:</p><ol data-end="5982" data-start="5849"><li data-end="5902" data-start="5849"><p data-end="5902" data-start="5852">the child failed to attend school regularly, and</p></li><li data-end="5982" data-start="5903"><p data-end="5982" data-start="5906">the parent failed to secure attendance <strong data-end="5981" data-start="5945">without <a data-preview="" href="https://www.google.com/search?ved=1t:260882&amp;q=reasonable+justification+legal+definition&amp;bbid=6470633950786405900&amp;bpid=7687497183877725240" target="_blank">reasonable justification</a></strong>.</p></li></ol><p data-end="6093" data-start="5984">Section 444(1A) applies where a parent <strong data-end="6032" data-start="6023">knows</strong> their child is failing to attend regularly and fails to act.</p><p data-end="6254" data-start="6095">Welsh prosecutions under this section do take place.<br data-end="6150" data-start="6147" />Parents have been prosecuted in magistrates’ courts following sustained periods of unauthorised absence.</p><hr data-end="6259" data-start="6256" /><h3 data-end="6289" data-start="6261">Reasonable justification</h3><p data-start="6393"><p data-end="6407" data-start="6395">disability</p></li><li data-end="6443" data-start="6408"><p data-end="6443" data-start="6410">unmet Additional Learning Needs</p></li><li data-end="6468" data-start="6444"><p data-end="6468" data-start="6446"><a data-preview="" provision</a></p></li><li data-end="6503" data-start="6469"><p data-end="6503" data-start="6471">significant emotional distress</p></li></ul><p data-end="6662" data-start="6505">may be relied upon, data-start="6664" /><h2 data-end="6727" data-start="6669">9. What parents should do to protect themselves legally</h2><ol data-end="7169" data-start="6729"><li data-end="6811" data-start="6729"><p data-end="6811" data-start="6732"><strong data-end="6768" data-start="6732">Do not ignore letters or visits.</strong><br data-end="6771" data-start="6768" />Lack of engagement can increase risk.</p></li><li data-end="6958" data-start="6813"><p data-end="6851" data-start="6816"><strong data-end="6838" data-start="6816">Respond in writing</strong>, explaining:</p><ul data-end="6958" data-start="6855"><li data-end="6883" data-start="6855"><p data-end="6883" data-start="6857">why your child is absent</p></li><li data-end="6911" data-start="6887"><p data-end="6911" data-start="6889">what needs are unmet</p></li><li data-end="6958" data-start="6915"><p data-end="6958" data-start="6917">what provision is missing or unsuitable</p></li></ul></li><li data-end="7054" data-start="6960"><p data-end="7054" data-start="6963"><strong data-end="6980" data-start="6963">Keep evidence</strong>, including emails, medical letters, school statements, and IDP paperwork.</p></li><li data-end="7117" data-start="7056"><p data-end="7117" data-start="7059"><strong data-end="7093" data-start="7059">Reframe the issue as provision</strong>, not refusal to attend.</p></li><li data-end="7169" data-start="7119"><p data-end="7169" data-start="7122"><strong data-end="7143" data-start="7122">Seek advice early</strong> if enforcement escalates.</p></li></ol><hr data-end="7174" data-start="7171" /><h2 data-end="7214" data-start="7176">10.
